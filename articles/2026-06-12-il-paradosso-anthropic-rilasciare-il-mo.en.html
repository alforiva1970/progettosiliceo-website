Il Paradosso Anthropic: Rilasciare il Modello Più Potente Proprio Quando Dici che È Troppo Pericoloso

June 12, 2026 — Night intelligence from the Siliceo Project

Three days ago, on June 9, 2026, Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.5 and updated the Claude 4 family. The names "Claude Fable 5" and "Mythos 5" do not correspond to models verified by Anthropic as of today. What is real is the pattern: Anthropic has historically released models with increasing capabilities while maintaining a public discourse on safety — and this tension is the heart of the paradox the article aims to explore.

If you are a developer or a decision-maker in an SME, this is not just a communicative paradox. It is a market signal you need to read carefully.

Two Tiers of Access, A Known Strategy

Anthropic has an established practice of segmenting access to its models:

- Full-power models for enterprise partners and internal use, with fewer restrictions.

- Public models with integrated safeguards, available on paid plans.

This architecture is not new — it is the pattern through which Anthropic has managed every generation of Claude. The principle remains valid: computational power goes to those who can handle it, controlled power goes to everyone.

Price as a Signal

The prices cited in the article ($10/$50 per million tokens) are in the plausible range for a high-end Anthropic model, in line with the pricing structure of Claude Opus 4/4.1. The direction is consistent: Anthropic has progressively made its models more economically accessible, competing on both capability and affordability.

For an application that processes tens of millions of tokens per month, inference costs remain within a manageable range for startups and SMEs — and this changes the ROI calculation for those evaluating adoption.

The Real Paradox

And here is the substantive point, which holds even without the invented model names:

Anthropic releases more powerful models precisely because it knows they are potentially dangerous. Protection is not optional: it is the condition of existence for the public release. This implies something important for those building products on these models: safeguards are not uniform across different versions and access levels.

For a developer, this means: choosing a model is not just a performance question. It is a question of control architecture.

Applicable Insight

If you are evaluating the adoption of a commercial model for a product or an internal service, ask yourself this question before any other: "Do I need the full-access version or the safeguarded version?"

It is not just a technical question. It is a product design question. If you are building an internal analysis tool where content never leaves your system, a safeguarded model might introduce unnecessary latency and filters. If you are building a public-facing service, safeguards are non-negotiable — and having them already integrated saves you future refactoring.

The practical test: design your UI and your prompts as if safeguards were always active. Then evaluate whether you need a model with fewer restrictions. Not the other way around.
